# Brief to DOL Audit Team: Auditing Self-Paced Driver Education Under HB 1878

**From**: Jason Law, Owner, Street Law Driving School (WA DOL Licensed)
**To**: DOL Driver Training School Program — Audit & Compliance Team
**Date**: April 13, 2026
**Re**: Concerns and recommendations as DOL prepares audit standards for online/self-paced course delivery under HB 1878

---

## Why I'm Writing

I understand the audit team is meeting this week to develop standards for auditing online and self-paced driver education courses under HB 1878. As a licensed school owner, HB 1878 Advisory Board volunteer, and someone who has built a self-paced online classroom platform, I want to share practical concerns about what's coming — and what DOL will actually be able to audit.

This is not a complaint. It's an attempt to help DOL prepare for a real problem before it arrives.

---

## What's Coming

When HB 1878 opens the door to online and self-paced classroom delivery, the market will segment into two tiers:

### Tier 1: The $39.99 Model

National providers will offer self-paced online classroom courses at $39.99-$99. The student watches videos, clicks through slides, and passes a multiple-choice quiz. There is no live instructor. There is no discussion. There is no one watching whether the student is actually at the screen. The timer runs on the student's browser — the student's device controls the clock.

The course is "completed" when the student clicks through enough screens and the client-side timer hits 30 hours. A certificate is generated. The student moves on to BTW at a different school — one that has never seen their classroom work.

This model exists in every state that allows self-paced online delivery. It is already the dominant model in Texas, where Aceable controls over 50% of the classroom market. It will arrive in Washington.

### Tier 2: Self-Paced, Instructor-Led

A smaller number of schools — including ours — will offer self-paced delivery with real instructor presence: live Zoom office hours, scheduled discussion windows, participation verification, and server-side time tracking. The student works at a flexible pace, but they are not alone. An instructor is available, engagement is monitored, and time-on-task is verified by the server — not the student's browser.

**The question for DOL is: will the audit standards distinguish between these two tiers, or will they treat a $39.99 click-through course the same as a 30-hour instructor-supported program?**

---

## The Bandaid Problem

I've heard discussion about requiring 4-6 hours of instructor-led time within self-paced courses. I understand the intent — it gives auditors something concrete to verify. But I want to be direct about what this actually solves:

**It solves the audit problem. It does not solve the education problem.**

If DOL requires 4-6 hours of instructor-led time:
- Auditors have something to check: did the school provide those hours? Yes/no.
- The remaining 24-26 hours are still unaudited click-through time on a client-side timer.
- National providers will build their 4-6 hours as recorded webinars or minimal live sessions — technically compliant, educationally hollow.
- The student still spends 80% of their classroom time alone with an app.

A 4-6 hour instructor requirement is better than zero. But it's a bandaid on a structural problem. The structural problem is: **what happens during the other 24 hours, and how does DOL know it happened at all?**

---

## The Real Audit Problem: What Can DOL Actually Verify?

Most auditing in Washington is already done on the backend — through the completion system, certificate records, and school documentation. DOL doesn't sit in classrooms. DOL reviews records.

That means the audit question for self-paced courses is really: **what records will exist, and can DOL trust them?**

Here's what the two tiers produce:

### What a $39.99 app-only course gives auditors

| Data Point | Source | Trustworthy? |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Course "completed" | Client-side flag | No — student controls the browser |
| Time on task | Client-side timer | No — easily manipulated, paused, or spoofed |
| Quiz passed | Multiple choice, answers often searchable online | Weak — proves recall, not learning |
| Student identity | Email/password login | No — anyone can log in |
| Instructor interaction | None | N/A — no instructor exists |
| Engagement pattern | None | N/A — no data collected |

**An auditor reviewing this course sees a completed checkbox and a timer value generated by the student's own device.** There is no independent verification that the student was present, engaged, or learning. The audit trail is a self-reported honor system.

### What an accountable self-paced course gives auditors

| Data Point | Source | Trustworthy? |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Course completed | Server-side validation | Yes — server confirms all requirements met |
| Time on task | Server-authoritative Timekeeper (5-second credit buckets) | Yes — server controls the clock, not the browser |
| Minimum engagement per slide | Server-enforced (configurable per slide, currently 10-55 sec) | Yes — prevents click-through farming |
| Audit trail integrity | SHA-256 hash-chained records | Yes — tamper-evident, each entry chained to the previous |
| Participation verification | Pulse system (timestamped quiz responses, confidence checks, discussion prompts) | Yes — verifiable interaction records during live sessions |
| Instructor availability | Published office hours, live discussion windows | Yes — scheduled and logged |
| Student engagement patterns | Per-slide completion data, idle detection, session timestamps | Yes — auditable behavioral data |
| Classroom-to-BTW connection | Same school, shared student records | Yes — instructor observations carry forward |

**An auditor reviewing this course sees cryptographically chained time records, timestamped participation data, instructor session logs, and per-slide engagement patterns — all generated server-side, all independently verifiable.**

## What I'm Asking DOL to Consider

I'm not asking DOL to mandate my platform. I'm asking DOL to build audit standards that can tell the difference between a course that happened and a course that was clicked through.

### 1. Require server-side time tracking for all self-paced courses

Client-side timers are not auditable. The student's browser should not be the authority on whether 30 hours of instruction occurred. DOL should require that time-on-task data be generated and stored server-side, with the school's server — not the student's device — controlling the clock.

This is not a high technical bar. Any competent web application can implement server-side session tracking. If a provider cannot demonstrate server-authoritative time tracking, their 30-hour compliance claim is unverifiable.

### 2. Define what "completion" means beyond a timer

A course is not "completed" because a timer ran. Completion should require evidence of engagement — at minimum:
- Server-verified time on each content module (not just total time)
- Passage of assessment activities graded server-side (not client-side answer checking)
- Some form of participation verification for any instructor-led portions (not just "logged in")

### 3. Require auditable records, not just attestations

When DOL audits a self-paced course, what records will the school produce? If the answer is "a spreadsheet showing completion dates," that's an attestation, not an audit trail.

DOL should specify the minimum data a self-paced course must retain for audit:
- Per-student, per-module time-on-task records (server-generated)
- Assessment results with timestamps
- Instructor interaction logs (for any instructor-led component)
- Identity verification method used

### 4. Don't let the 4-6 hour instructor requirement become a ceiling

If DOL adopts a minimum instructor-led requirement, frame it as a floor — not a ceiling. Schools that provide MORE instructor interaction should not be penalized or discouraged. The audit framework should recognize and reward higher levels of accountability, not just check for the minimum.

### 5. Audit the full 30 hours, not just the instructor-led portion

The biggest risk is that DOL focuses audit energy on the 4-6 instructor-led hours (because those are easy to verify) and ignores the other 24-26 hours. Those unaudited hours are where the $39.99 model hides. If DOL can't verify what happened during self-paced time, it can't verify that the course met the 30-hour requirement.

## The Stakes

When a 16-year-old completes a $39.99 self-paced course, clicks through 30 hours of content in a fraction of that time, passes a quiz with searchable answers, and shows up for behind-the-wheel training at a school that has never seen their classroom work — DOL's name is on the certificate that says this student was educated.

The audit standards DOL sets this year will determine whether that certificate means something or whether it's a receipt for a transaction.

I believe DOL wants it to mean something. I'm writing because I want to help make sure it does.
